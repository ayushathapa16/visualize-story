# The Story, and What's Actually Data

Two things this document does:

1. Tells the whole story, frame by frame, as it currently plays.
2. States **exactly which numbers are real, which are sourced, and which visuals are
   illustrative** - because a paper-cut chart looks like evidence whether or not it is any.

---

# Part 1 - The Story

**One Bird's Story. Toronto's Future.** - 26 frames, 7 acts, told by Willow, a Tree Swallow.

The argument the whole piece is making, in one line: *nature runs on timing, warming breaks the
timing, and a number small enough to sound harmless is large enough to empty a city's sky.*

## Act I - Home (1–4)

**1 · Darkness.** Black screen, wind, one swallow call. "Every spring, millions of birds return to
Toronto. This is the story of one of them."

**2 · The Journey.** A migration path draws itself from South America up through Central America
and the United States, across Lake Ontario, to Toronto. Willow flies it. "I fly thousands of
kilometres to spend my summer in Toronto."

**3 · Meet Your Summer Neighbour.** Paper-cut Toronto - CN Tower, waterfront, wetlands, parks,
people. She flies through it. "Toronto is your home. It's mine too." *This is the frame that makes
the city shared, and it is why the ending works.*

**4 · My Routine.** A season timeline: April → July. Arrive, build, lay, raise. Nothing is wrong
yet. "Nature always knew what came next."

## Act II - Nature's Clock (5–7)

**5 · Nature's Clock.** The iconic image: meshed gears, each one an event - flowers bloom, insects
emerge, the swallow arrives, eggs hatch, chicks are fed. Icons first, labels only after you've
understood the system. Everything turns in sync.

**6 · A Good Year.** The nest. Four chicks hatch. The sky is thick with insects, the parents come
back loaded. This is what a working year looks like - and the audience has to *feel* it, or the
loss later means nothing.

**7 · The First Crack.** One gear slips. Gradually. A crack draws across the clock. No explanation.
"But something has changed."

## Act III - The Clock Breaks (8–14)

**8 · Spring Arrives Earlier.** A thermometer climbs **+1 °C → +2 °C**. Trees bloom, flowers open,
insects emerge - and Willow is still out over the water, flying north. "Spring is arriving before I
do." *Remember this thermometer. Frame 20 comes back for it.*

**9 · The Timeline Shifts.** The strongest chart in the piece. Four bars - flowers, insects, bird,
chicks - all overlapping. Then **only the insect bar slides earlier**. The overlap opens into a gap.
Only once the gap is undeniable does the term appear: **phenological mismatch**.

**10 · I Have to Hurry.** She lands into a spring already in progress and starts nesting at once.
"If I wait, my chicks may miss the food they need."

**11 · Then Everything Changes.** Dark clouds, cold rain, snow. The insects stop flying. The sky
goes empty. Silence.

**12 · Hungry Chicks.** The parents search, return with one insect, then none. The chicks go quiet.
"My babies are hungry."

**13 · The Chain Reaction.** Cold → insects stop → parents search longer → less food → slower
growth → lower survival.

**14 · Searching Farther.** Top-down. The search radius around the nest expands, and expands. Each
trip is longer; she comes home slower. "Every extra minute searching for food is one less minute
feeding my family."

## Act IV - A Gamble (15–17)

**15 · Every Spring is a Guess.** A weather wheel - warm, cold, rain, heat - spins and never lands.
Willow waits beside it. "Every spring… I have to guess, and hope I guessed right."

**16 · Interactive Choice.** *The one clickable moment in the piece.* **Should Willow lay her eggs
now?** - *Lay eggs now* / *Wait longer*.

- Lay now → the eggs hatch straight into a cold snap. The insects stop. Hungry chicks.
- Wait → no cold snap, but the insect boom peaks and drains away while she's still on eggs. She
  hatches into an empty sky. Hungry chicks.

**Both answers lose, and that is the entire point.** The audience isn't being tested; they're being
shown that the choice itself has stopped working. (Scroll past without clicking and it plays the
first path anyway.)

**17 · No Perfect Choice.** The wheel finally stops. Nothing is resolved. *"Breeding has become a
gamble."*

## Act V - I'm Not Alone (18–21)

**18 · Zoom Out.** The camera pulls back off Willow. One bird becomes several, becomes many, becomes
a skyline full of them. "My story isn't unique."

**19 · Toronto's Bird Neighbours.** Six species as cards: Tree Swallow, Canada Jay, Common Loon,
Bobolink, Wood Thrush, Barn Swallow. Names and faces, no statistics (see Part 2 for why). "None of
us makes the journey alone. We all keep the same appointment with spring."

**20 · Two Degrees.** **The turn - and the frame the whole piece was built toward.**

Frame 8's thermometer returns. The audience recognises it: it's the one that read +1 °C / +2 °C, and
they'd already decided that was a small number.

Then its scale is **re-labelled** - to **+1.5 °C** and **+3.0 °C**, the warming scenarios science
actually models.

> *One degree. Two degrees.*
> *It sounds like nothing at all.*

The mercury climbs to +1.5 °C. Ontario's bird ranges dim and creep north. It holds there - that's
the survivable outcome.

Then it climbs to +3.0 °C. The ranges collapse and pull away from Toronto entirely.

> *It is enough to put hundreds of birds in danger.*

Only now, with the map having already made the argument, does the number appear: **389 of 604**
North American bird species are vulnerable at +3 °C. Hold warming to +1.5 °C and **nearly 150** of
them are no longer at risk.

**21 · A Different Sound.** Split screen, one shared skyline. Left: today, Willow, her song. Right:
future Toronto, different birds, a visibly different song-shape. "A warmer world doesn't only change
where we live. It changes what your city sounds like."

## Act VI - The Future (22–23)

**22 · The Empty Nest.** The same nest, the same branch, at sunset. Empty. The frame holds on the
emptiness long enough to hurt - and then one swallow glides in and lands. "I come back hoping
Toronto still feels like home."

**23 · My Children.** The fledglings take their first flight and dissolve into the skyline - flying
*into* the city, not away from it. "I hope my children will too."

## Act VII - A Shared Home (24–26)

**24 · This City Belongs to All of Us.** The park, the people, the birds overhead, one frame.
"Toronto belongs to all of us."

**25 · What You Can Do.** Four cards, each a small causal chain - the same grammar the story has used
all along: plant native wildflowers → more insects → more food; leave fallen leaves; reduce
pesticides; volunteer at Tommy Thompson Park.

**26 · Closing Scene.** Golden sunset. Willow crosses the skyline one last time. Fade to black - and
the swallow call from Frame 1 returns, in the dark, except now you know whose it is.

> Toronto is your home.
> It's ours too.
> The future of Toronto's birds depends on the choices we make today.

# Part 2 - Is the Data Real?

**Short answer: the numbers stated on screen are real and sourced. The charts and maps are
illustrative, and are deliberately never labelled with numbers they can't support.**

The piece makes exactly **one** hard quantitative claim, in Frame 20. Everything else is either a
real scientific *concept* rendered as a picture, or a qualitative statement.

## The numbers that appear on screen (Frame 20) - REAL

All from the National Audubon Society, *Survival by Degrees: 389 Bird Species on the Brink* (2019).

| Claim on screen | Status |
|---|---|
| **389 of 604** North American bird species are vulnerable at **+3 °C** | ✅ Audubon's headline finding (that's the 64% / "two-thirds of North American birds" figure) |
| Vulnerable birds **"may lose more than half their range"** | ✅ Audubon's own definition of moderately/highly vulnerable |
| Hold warming to **+1.5 °C** → **nearly 150** species are no longer vulnerable | ✅ Audubon (they also state 76% of vulnerable species are better off at 1.5 °C) |
| The scenarios **+1.5 / +2.0 / +3.0 °C** | ✅ These are the three scenarios Audubon actually models - the thermometer isn't inventing a scale |

Sources:
- https://www.audubon.org/climate/survivalbydegrees
- https://www.audubon.org/climate/survivalbydegrees/state/ca/on (the Ontario page)

These live in **one place** in the code - `SCENARIOS` in `src/components/climate.js` - so they can be
corrected in a single edit.

## What is NOT data (and is never presented as data)

**The Ontario map's shrinking blobs are illustrative.** The five coloured range-blobs shrink and
retreat north as the mercury rises. Those shapes are *paper-cut, not projected*. They are not
traced from Audubon's range models, and no number is ever attached to them. Their job is to make the
sourced sentence legible - *"lose more than half their range"* is an abstraction until you watch a
range lose half of itself and pull off your city.

The per-species shrink amounts (`loss15` / `loss30` in `climate.js`) are hand-tuned for readability.
**Do not quote them.** They're marked as illustrative in the source file.

**Frame 19's bird cards carry no statistics - on purpose.** Audubon does publish per-species
vulnerability, but the Ontario figures sit inside an interactive widget that couldn't be read
directly, and a card is precisely the place where an invented category ("Highly Vulnerable") would
be mistaken for a fact. So the cards show a name and a face, and the sourced, aggregate claim is
left to Frame 20. One scientific idea per frame.

**Frame 9's phenological mismatch is a real, well-established phenomenon** - the insect emergence
peak shifting earlier than the birds that depend on it - but the four bars and the size of the gap
are schematic, not measured. The chart teaches the concept; it doesn't report a study.

**Frame 8's +1 °C / +2 °C thermometer** is narrative framing (warming, generally), not a citation of
a specific Toronto temperature record.

**Frame 16's two outcomes** are a dramatisation of a real bind (lay early and risk a cold snap; lay
late and miss the insect peak). The specific outcomes aren't a simulation.

## One thing deliberately left out

There is a 2026 study out of **Long Point, Ontario** with striking, very on-point figures about tree
swallows and collapsing insect numbers. Its source returned an HTTP 403 and the numbers could not be
verified first-hand, so **they are not in the piece.** If you can confirm them from the paper, they
would strengthen Frame 20 considerably - Ontario-specific evidence about *this exact species* is
better than continental evidence about birds in general.

## The rule this project follows

From `docs/direction.md`: *show before explaining; the visuals should explain 80% of the story.*
That gives an honesty dividend. Because the animation carries the argument, the text only has to
carry the **one number that is actually true** - and that number arrives last, after the picture has
already convinced you.

If a number can't be sourced, it doesn't go on screen. The picture can still do the work.
