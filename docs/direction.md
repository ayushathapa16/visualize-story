# Story & Animation Direction Guide
## Willow - *One Bird's Story. Toronto's Future.*

---

# Creative Vision

This project should feel less like an animated infographic and more like a short animated documentary.

The audience should **experience** climate change through Willow's life before they learn the scientific explanation behind it.

Think of the emotional pacing of Pixar, the editorial storytelling of The New York Times, and the visual craftsmanship of The Pudding.

The objective is **not** to teach climate science.

The objective is to make people care enough about Willow that they naturally want to understand why her world is changing.

---

# Core Storytelling Philosophy

Every frame should answer an emotional question before introducing scientific information.

The audience should experience Willow's world first, then discover why it changed.

### Golden Rules

- Show before explaining.
- Animate before labeling.
- Emotion before data.
- One scientific idea per frame.
- Motion should always serve the story.
- Every visualization answers a question the audience already has.
- Never overwhelm the audience with text.
- The visuals should explain 80% of the story.

# Motion Hierarchy

Every scene should have three levels of motion.

## Primary Motion

The one thing the audience should immediately notice.

Examples

- Willow flying
- Gear rotating
- Timeline shifting
- Thermometer rising

---

## Secondary Motion

Supports the story.

Examples

- Trees blooming
- Flowers opening
- Chicks moving
- Rain falling

---

## Ambient Motion

Creates life.

Examples

- Clouds
- Water
- Grass
- Floating leaves
- Gentle bird movement

Everything should not animate equally.

The eye should always know where to look.

# Animation Language

Positive

- Upward movement
- Warm colors
- Smooth easing
- Bright lighting
- Faster motion

Negative

- Downward movement
- Desaturation
- Hesitation
- Longer pauses
- Slower movement

Nature

- Organic curves
- Slight randomness
- Gentle oscillation

Human

- Straight lines
- Structured layouts
- Clean typography

This keeps every animation visually consistent.

# Memory Test

For every frame ask

> If the narration were muted,
> would someone still understand the story?

If the answer is

No

The animation isn't communicating enough.

The visuals should tell the story.

Narration should simply deepen it.

---

# Success Criteria

The audience should finish the story remembering these ideas:

✓ Toronto is home to birds too.

✓ Willow is one of many migratory birds that depend on Toronto.

✓ Nature depends on precise seasonal timing.

✓ Climate change is breaking that timing.

✓ Willow's story reflects a much larger change affecting Toronto's bird community.

✓ Small seasonal shifts can have enormous ecological consequences.

✓ Climate change is not only changing temperatures.

It is changing what Toronto looks like,

what Toronto sounds like,

and who calls Toronto home.

---

# Final Creative Goal

When someone reaches the last frame, they shouldn't think:

> "I learned about phenological mismatch."

They should think:

> "I'll never hear a Tree Swallow the same way again."

If the audience leaves with that feeling, then the visualization has succeeded.
